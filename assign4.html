Big takeaways/ Reflection

The five tenets (Inspiring Wonder, Painting a Picture, Application, Connecting the Past to the Present, and Facilitating Change) of this book provided a really helpful framework for me to think about the ways that I want to structure units and my classroom/ lessons broadly. It was helpful to read about examples of ways that each of these tenets could be incorporated into the classroom. However, I found a decent amount of this book kind of irrelevant because either the suggestions were not aligned with my age range, vision, or needs as a high school teacher. I also think that some of the suggestions were not as productive as they claim to be. Mostly the arcs where teachers asked young people to simulate discrimination or oppression. I think that those can be interesting and maybe build empathy. However, I do not think it is worth the trauma that they can inflict on the marginalized students in the classroom. That is something I felt was missing from the book, broadly as well. I think that the considerations of the different ways that students access information and content about oppression is far from universal, and I get wanting to build empathy for all human beings, but I think that you need to prioritize the marginalized students in the classroom. Particularly, if the other option is to “see the humanity” in past’s oppressors.

Overall, this book with its focus on younger grades, and somewhat weird suggestions for activities was not super exciting to me. However, the framework I think has a lot of value in it and I will certainly be thinking about that in my teaching practices.
